ZERO PADDING
INCREASING DFT RESOLUTION

Thereâ€™s a classic trick you need to be aware of when working with the Discrete Fourier Transform. When you need higher resolution in your output - a more fine grained view of the frequency domain - you can choose to append a string of zeroes to the end of your input signal. This doesn't change the frequency content of the input signal, but merely interpolates or "fills in" the output spectrum. You can convince yourself of this fact by increasing the zero-padding factor using the slider underneath Figure 1.

Often you need to do this sort of zero-padding to ensure that the input you provide to an FFT (Fast Fourier Transform) routine has a length which is equal to a power of two. Rest assured that you are not altering the frequency content of your signal in any undue way. You must simply ensure that you perform any windowing before zero-padding your signal.
